Introduction

Over the years Gerrymandering, or the redistribution of district lines to favor a party or class, has been a high topic of debate. According to Barasch, some cases of gerrymandering have been found as far back as 1812; one by Governor Elbridge Gerry in Massachusetts to manipulating a district to look like a salamander.(Barasch 2) Over the years many politicians have “gerrymandered” districts, with this tradition continuing every 10 years to this current day. Today the three main themes I have been finding on gerrymandering are about the misconceptions of gerrymandering, the popular solution to gerrymandering: Math, and lastly answering the main question of gerrymandering: is it good for our political system. Although the literature represents gerrymandering in different lights, this paper’s primary focus will be on how we as a society should deal with gerrymandering and many different opinions on gerrymandering itself.

Explanations and Misunderstandings of Gerrymandering

As Americans we tend to take things as how we hear it in newspapers, internet, and magazines; when it comes to gerrymandering we always think that gerrymandering is bad. In the article Gerrymandering Isn’t Evil by John Sides and Eric McGhee they make a very valid point that there are two types of gerrymandering. The first is called partisan gerrymandering which involves “redrawing district lines with the intention of increasing the number of seats for one party.”(Sides & McGhee ) This is the method that most people hear about, but there is another way that gerrymandering still takes place. The second way is called bipartisan gerrymandering which “seeks to protect incumbents of both parties by making every district safer for either a Democrat or a Republican.”(Sides & McGhee ). We as American people would seek to have “safe” elections where there is neither packing, separating opposing parties across many districts, nor cracking, putting as many opposing party votes in one district as possible , in drawing district lines. Another misconception is pointed out in the article Gerrymandering Isn’t a Republican Problem by Philip Devoe that a “fair shape” is important to the people when deciding where the boundaries are drawn. This overall is not a great way to determine districts at all because normal shapes in itself are gerrymandering too. A great example of this is how many democrats usually crowd in the cities so it would be easy to round them up in one district, but since it would be in a largely populated city it would be much more unfair for democrats to win since there are many more districts for other parties to win. Overall I believe that people do have misconceptions about how gerrymandering is performed, but I think these are a few that are good to clear up.
